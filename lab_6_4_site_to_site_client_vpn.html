1. Lab Purpose

The purpose of this lab is to help students understand how VPNs extend protected network access across an untrusted or less-trusted network. Students will configure and test either a remote-access/client VPN or a site-to-site VPN design using pfSense and local virtual machines. The lab emphasizes configuration documentation, traffic-flow verification, firewall rules, and log-based troubleshooting.

Main goal: Students should understand that a VPN is not just encryption. A working VPN also requires identity, tunnel addressing, routing, firewall rules, and verification that only intended resources are reachable.

2. Learning Objectives

  1. Explain the difference between a client VPN and a site-to-site VPN.
  2. Identify VPN tunnel networks, protected subnets, endpoints, and allowed traffic flows.
  3. Configure a pfSense-based VPN design using an instructor-approved VPN method.
  4. Create VPN firewall rules that follow least-privilege principles.
  5. Verify VPN tunnel establishment using status pages, client output, and logs.
  6. Test connectivity from the VPN side to permitted internal resources.
  7. Test that unauthorized network access remains blocked.
  8. Use ping, curl, traceroute, nmap, and logs to verify behavior.
  9. Document routes, firewall rules, allowed resources, blocked resources, and remaining risk.
  10. Explain OS-specific VPN client and virtualization differences.

3. Lab Scenario

A small organization needs secure remote access to internal lab services. The security team is considering two designs. The first design uses a client VPN so an individual remote analyst can connect to the internal lab network. The second design uses a site-to-site VPN so two networks can communicate through an encrypted tunnel. Your task is to implement the instructor-assigned option, verify that intended traffic works, verify that unnecessary traffic is blocked, and document the VPN design.

6. Safety Rules

  1. Use only the local virtual lab or instructor-provided VPN endpoint.
  2. Do not expose VPN services to public networks unless the instructor explicitly authorizes it.
  3. Do not share private keys, certificates, pre-shared keys, or exported client profiles in the final submission.
  4. Use lab-only credentials and lab-only certificates.
  5. Do not scan or connect to non-lab systems through the VPN.
  6. Use least-privilege firewall rules for VPN traffic.
  7. Take a pfSense snapshot before VPN changes.
  8. Sanitize secrets from screenshots before submission if necessary.
Important: VPN configuration files and keys are sensitive. Submit evidence that the VPN works, but do not submit private keys or reusable credentials.

7. Key Concepts

Remote Access / Client VPN

A client VPN allows an individual device to connect securely to a protected network. The client receives a tunnel address and can reach resources permitted by routing and firewall rules.

Site-to-Site VPN

A site-to-site VPN connects two networks through a tunnel. Each side must know the remote endpoint, remote subnet, and security parameters.

Tunnel Network

The tunnel network is the logical network used by the VPN connection itself. It is different from the LAN and DMZ subnets.

Protected Networks

Protected networks are the internal subnets reachable through the VPN, such as a LAN or DMZ subnet.

Split Tunnel vs. Full Tunnel

In a split tunnel, only selected routes use the VPN. In a full tunnel, most or all client traffic is sent through the VPN. This lab uses split-tunnel thinking unless the instructor specifies otherwise.

Firewall Rules for VPN

A VPN tunnel does not automatically mean full trust. Firewall rules should define exactly what VPN users or remote sites can access.

VPN ElementMeaningExample
EndpointSystem that terminates the tunnelpfSense WAN address
Tunnel networkAddress space used by VPN clients or tunnel peers10.10.99.0/24
Protected subnetInternal network reachable through VPN10.10.20.0/24
Peer/client identityCertificate, key, user, or peer definitionVPN client profile or peer public key

16. Instructor Notes

Core lesson: A VPN should be treated as a controlled access path, not a magic trusted bridge. Students should prove both the tunnel and the access policy.
  1. Choose one VPN method for the class to reduce troubleshooting complexity.
  2. Use client VPN if students have only one pfSense VM.
  3. Use site-to-site VPN only if students have a second firewall/router endpoint or a shared instructor endpoint.
  4. Provide a clear tunnel network and allowed resource list before students begin.
  5. Require students to hide or omit private keys, certificates, and profile secrets from submissions.
  6. Grade both positive tests and negative tests: allowed access and blocked access.
