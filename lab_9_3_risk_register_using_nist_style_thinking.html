1. Lab Purpose

The purpose of this lab is to help students convert technical findings from earlier labs into an organized cybersecurity risk register. Students will identify assets, threats, vulnerabilities, controls, likelihood, impact, risk level, risk response, residual risk, and risk ownership.

Main goal: Students should learn that risk management connects technical security findings to business decisions. A risk register helps an organization prioritize what to fix, who owns the risk, and how progress will be tracked.

2. Learning Objectives

  1. Explain the purpose of a cybersecurity risk register.
  2. Identify assets, threats, vulnerabilities, impacts, controls, and risk owners.
  3. Use NIST-style thinking to connect threat sources, threat events, vulnerabilities, predisposing conditions, likelihood, and impact.
  4. Create a simple likelihood and impact scoring model.
  5. Calculate initial risk and residual risk using a consistent method.
  6. Map findings from previous labs to risk register entries.
  7. Recommend risk responses such as mitigate, accept, transfer, avoid, or monitor.
  8. Assign risk ownership and due dates.
  9. Prioritize the top cybersecurity risks for a small organization.
  10. Create an executive summary that translates technical risks into business language.
  11. Compare risk register needs across Linux endpoints, Windows endpoints, cloud services, network devices, containers, and backup systems.

3. Lab Scenario

You are working with a small organization that has completed several security assessments: network mapping, firewall configuration, SSH hardening, SIEM/IDS alert review, endpoint hardening, cloud IAM policy review, container security review, backup/restore testing, incident response planning, and digital evidence collection. Management now wants a risk register that summarizes the most important risks and recommends a practical treatment plan.

4. NIST-Style Risk Model

Risk Register Development Flow Organization Context | v Asset Identification | v Threat Sources and Threat Events | v Vulnerabilities and Predisposing Conditions | v Existing Controls | v Likelihood Assessment | v Impact Assessment | v Initial Risk Rating | v Recommended Controls and Risk Response | v Residual Risk Rating | v Risk Owner, Due Date, Status, Review Schedule NIST-style thinking used in this lab: - What asset is at risk? - What threat source or threat event could affect it? - What vulnerability or weakness makes the event more likely or more damaging? - What existing controls reduce the risk? - How likely is the event? - What would the impact be? - What is the current risk level? - What response is appropriate? - What controls should be added or improved? - What risk remains after treatment? - Who owns the risk? - When should it be reviewed?

6. Safety Rules

  1. Use only lab findings, sample findings, or instructor-approved information.
  2. Do not include real personal data, passwords, tokens, private keys, or confidential organizational information.
  3. Do not run new scans or tests outside the assigned lab environment.
  4. Do not exaggerate risk ratings without evidence.
  5. Do not claim a system is compromised unless evidence supports that conclusion.
  6. Clearly separate facts, assumptions, and recommendations.
  7. Use fictional role names if real risk owners should not be identified.
  8. Treat the risk register as sensitive because it identifies weaknesses and priorities.
Important: A risk register can reveal security weaknesses. It should be protected and shared only with authorized stakeholders.

7. Key Concepts

Risk

Risk is the possibility that a threat event could exploit a vulnerability or weakness and cause negative impact to an asset, system, user, organization, or mission.

Asset

An asset is something of value that needs protection. Examples include endpoints, servers, user accounts, cloud resources, firewall configurations, backups, data, and business services.

Threat Source

A threat source is where a threat may come from, such as an external attacker, insider, malware, system failure, misconfiguration, natural event, or user error.

Threat Event

A threat event is something that could happen, such as unauthorized login, ransomware file encryption, exposed service exploitation, cloud key misuse, or backup deletion.

Vulnerability

A vulnerability is a weakness that could be exploited or could increase the likelihood or impact of a threat event.

Predisposing Condition

A predisposing condition is a situation that makes a risk more likely or more serious, such as lack of monitoring, weak backup protection, poor documentation, or too many administrators.

Likelihood

Likelihood estimates how probable it is that the threat event will occur, considering threat capability, exposure, vulnerabilities, and existing controls.

Impact

Impact estimates the harm if the threat event occurs. Impact may affect confidentiality, integrity, availability, safety, cost, operations, reputation, or compliance.

Initial Risk and Residual Risk

Initial risk is the risk before new treatment actions. Residual risk is the risk expected to remain after recommended controls or treatments are applied.

Part 7 — Create the Likelihood Scoring Model

Create a simple 1–5 likelihood model.

nano ~/Lab9-3-RiskRegister/scoring/likelihood-scoring-model.txt
Lab 9.3 Likelihood Scoring Model

1 — Rare:
- Threat event is unlikely under current conditions.
- Strong controls are in place.
- Little exposure or few opportunities.

2 — Unlikely:
- Threat event could occur but is not expected.
- Some exposure exists.
- Controls reduce probability.

3 — Possible:
- Threat event is plausible.
- Exposure or weakness exists.
- Controls are partial or inconsistently applied.

4 — Likely:
- Threat event is expected or has clear opportunity.
- Weakness is significant.
- Controls are weak, missing, or untested.

5 — Almost Certain:
- Threat event is highly likely or already occurring.
- Asset is exposed.
- Controls are missing or ineffective.

Likelihood factors:
- Exposure to network or internet
- Known weakness present
- Threat activity common
- Asset attractiveness
- User behavior
- Control strength
- History of similar events
- Ease of exploitation
- Monitoring/detection coverage

Lab 9.3 Impact Scoring Model

1 — Negligible:
- Minimal disruption.
- No sensitive data involved.
- Easy recovery.
- Little or no cost.

2 — Minor:
- Limited user or system impact.
- Low sensitivity data.
- Short recovery time.
- Minor operational inconvenience.

3 — Moderate:
- One important system or group affected.
- Some sensitive data or business process impact.
- Recovery requires coordinated effort.
- Noticeable cost or downtime.

4 — Major:
- Critical asset affected.
- Significant data, operational, financial, or reputational impact.
- Recovery may require extended effort.
- Management involvement required.

5 — Severe:
- Organization-wide disruption.
- Sensitive data exposure or major outage.
- Legal/regulatory consequences possible.
- Major recovery cost or mission failure.

- Confidentiality impact

- Availability impact
- Safety impact
- Financial impact

- Legal or compliance impact

15. Troubleshooting Guide

ProblemLikely CauseFix
Risk entries sound too vagueAsset, threat event, or vulnerability is missingUse the structure: asset + threat event + weakness + impact.
Everything is rated criticalScoring model not applied consistentlyUse likelihood and impact definitions; reserve critical for the highest risks.
Risks are actually controlsConfusing treatment with risk statementWrite the risk first, then write the recommended control.
No evidence listedPrior lab findings not connectedReference Nmap, Wazuh, Suricata, endpoint hardening, backup, IAM, or container evidence.
Residual risk is the same as initial riskTreatment does not reduce likelihood or impactExplain how the control reduces likelihood, impact, or both.
Risk owner is missingOwnership not assignedUse role-based owners such as System Administrator, Network Administrator, Cloud Administrator, or Management.
Executive summary is too technicalCopied technical register textTranslate into plain language about business impact and priorities.
Risk register is too long but shallowToo many low-quality entriesFocus on at least ten complete entries with strong justification.

16. Instructor Notes

Core lesson: This lab should help students connect hands-on technical observations to structured risk management. A good risk register explains what can go wrong, why it matters, what controls exist, what should be done next, and who owns the decision.
  1. Allow students to use findings from earlier labs or provide a sample findings packet.
  2. Emphasize that risk scoring is judgment-based but must be justified.
  3. Encourage students to use role-based risk owners rather than real personal names.
  4. Grade consistency and reasoning more heavily than exact numerical scores.
  5. Discuss management acceptance of residual risk.
  6. Use this lab to prepare students for the final audit/reporting lab.
