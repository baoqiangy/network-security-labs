Lab 3.4 — Vulnerability Identification and Scan Reporting

Network Security hands-on lab based on the Lab 1.1 virtual environment. Students use safe, authorized evidence collection to identify potential weaknesses, distinguish verified findings from assumptions, and prepare a concise vulnerability-style report for the Ubuntu-Target VM.

1. Lab Purpose

This lab introduces vulnerability identification and professional scan reporting without exploitation. Students will collect service, version, configuration, and exposure evidence from the approved Ubuntu-Target VM and organize the evidence into findings with risk ratings and remediation recommendations.

Kali-Lab ───── Host-only / Private Network ───── Ubuntu-Target
Main goal: Students should learn that vulnerability analysis requires evidence, scope, cautious interpretation, and clear recommendations — not just tool output.

2. Learning Objectives

  1. Explain the difference between a vulnerability, exposure, misconfiguration, and observation.
  2. Collect safe service and version evidence from the approved Ubuntu-Target VM.
  3. Use Nmap selected-port scans to support vulnerability-style documentation.
  4. Use local Ubuntu commands to verify service state and package versions.
  5. Identify potential risk from unnecessary exposed services and service information leakage.
  6. Distinguish confirmed findings from assumptions that require further validation.
  7. Assign a simple qualitative risk rating based on likelihood and impact.
  8. Write remediation recommendations for common service-exposure issues.
  9. Prepare a professional vulnerability-style report with scope, evidence, findings, and cleanup notes.
  10. Explain OS-specific differences for Windows, Intel Mac, Apple Silicon Mac, Kali, and Ubuntu environments.

3. Lab Scenario

A security analyst has completed network discovery, port scanning, and service enumeration. The next task is to turn evidence into a short vulnerability-style report. The analyst must review exposed services on Ubuntu-Target, identify what could become a security concern, rate each finding, and recommend practical remediation steps.

6. Safety Rules

  1. Assess only the assigned Ubuntu-Target VM.
  2. Do not scan, test, or enumerate public systems, school networks, home routers, cloud systems, or classmates’ machines.
  3. Do not use bridged networking.
  4. Do not run exploit tools, vulnerability exploitation scripts, password attacks, or denial-of-service tools.
  5. Use only selected service checks shown in this lab.
  6. Do not claim a confirmed vulnerability unless the lab evidence supports it.
  7. Do not submit passwords, private keys, or sensitive personal information.
  8. Stop temporary services at the end of the lab.
Important: This is a vulnerability identification and reporting lab, not an exploitation lab.

7. Key Concepts

Vulnerability vs. Exposure vs. Observation

TermMeaningExample in This Lab
VulnerabilityA weakness that may be exploited to harm confidentiality, integrity, or availabilityOutdated vulnerable package, if verified by evidence
ExposureA service, port, banner, or detail visible to another systemSSH visible on TCP/22
MisconfigurationA configuration choice that increases riskUnnecessary temporary HTTP service left running
ObservationA neutral evidence-based note that may or may not be riskyHTTP server returns a Server header

Risk Rating

This lab uses a simple qualitative rating: Low, Medium, or High. Students should justify ratings using evidence, likelihood, impact, and lab context.

Evidence-Based Reporting

A good security finding includes the issue, affected asset, evidence, risk explanation, recommendation, and validation or cleanup note.

16. Instructor Notes

Core lesson: Vulnerability reporting is an evidence-based communication skill. Students should avoid overstating findings and should distinguish exposures, observations, misconfigurations, and confirmed vulnerabilities.

Recommended Teaching Sequence

  1. Review scope and the difference between vulnerability identification and exploitation.
  2. Collect service evidence for SSH and temporary HTTP.
  3. Collect local and remote evidence for each service.
  4. Review package/update status and SSH logs.
  5. Classify findings as vulnerability, exposure, misconfiguration, or observation.
  6. Assign simple risk ratings and justify them.
  7. Stop temporary services and verify cleanup.
  8. Complete a short vulnerability-style report.

Recommended Short Version

  1. Document assessment scope.
  2. Verify SSH exposure and configuration evidence.
  3. Start and test temporary HTTP on port 8080.
  4. Collect Nmap and curl evidence.
  5. Collect package/update and SSH log evidence.
  6. Classify three findings with risk ratings.
  7. Stop HTTP and submit the final report.
